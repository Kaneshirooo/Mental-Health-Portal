This chapter presents the methods and procedures used by the developers in conducting the study. 3.1 Methods
The methodology refers to the systematic approach employed in conducting research, encompassing the strategies, processes, and tools used in the collection, analysis, and interpretation of data to effectively address research questions and objectives. In this study, the methodology section outlines the structured procedures used to develop and assess the Guidance System for Pangasinan State University San Carlos Campus (PSUSC), ensuring the reliability and validity of outcomes.
To develop the PSUSC Web-Based Guidance System, the Agile methodology was adopted. Agile emphasizes adaptability, user involvement, and iterative development, making it well-suited for systems that must respond to evolving requirements and user feedback. In the context of educational institutions, where guidance services must adapt to student concerns, academic shifts, and institutional goals, Agile provides the flexibility needed for responsive system development.
According to Spiegler et al. (2021), Agile methodologies support continuous refinement through incremental improvements, allowing systems to evolve based on real-time user input and changing organizational needs. This approach minimizes risk and ensures that functional requirements are addressed early in the development process.
In a case study by Martínez-Muñoz et al. (2021), Agile practices were applied to an academic management platform, where the iterative cycles enabled developers to identify usability challenges early and respond with rapid adjustments. Such outcomes are particularly relevant for guidance systems that serve diverse student populations with varying technological proficiency and support needs.
Additionally, Santos et al. (2023) found that Agile enhances cross-functional collaboration and user-centered design, particularly in education-related platforms. Their findings support the use of Agile in projects requiring close coordination between developers, administrators, and end-users—such as guidance counselors and students.
By adopting Agile, the development team was able to maintain continuous user engagement, incorporate feedback during each phase, and prioritize feature delivery based on user needs. This resulted in a more robust, accessible, and effective system, capable of supporting both administrative guidance functions and student services dynamically.

Requirements. In the first phase, requirements gathering is approached through iterative collaboration with stakeholders to ensure the evolving needs of users are captured accurately. For a university guidance system, this adaptability is essential due to the varied and changing academic, psychological, and career concerns of students. Agile facilitates the use of user stories, journey maps, and visual models to translate needs into actionable features. According to Spiegler, Heinecke, and Heine (2021), Agile practices in requirement engineering increase clarity and reduce ambiguity by involving end users continuously, resulting in higher satisfaction and fewer misunderstandings during development. This is particularly beneficial for educational institutions, where inclusivity and responsiveness are critical.
Design. This phase emphasizes early and continuous design iterations, allowing real users—students and counselors in this case—to provide feedback throughout the process. Incorporating User-Centered Design (UCD) principles ensures that the guidance platform is intuitive, accessible, and inclusive for diverse users. As reported by Martínez-Muñoz et al. (2021), integrating UCD within Agile environments enables developers to identify usability issues early, improve design decisions, and build systems aligned with users’ actual needs. This is especially valuable in university settings where technological literacy and access levels vary among users.
Development.Agile’s iterative development approach—delivering software in manageable sprints—fits well with the modular requirements of a university guidance system. Features like chat functions, appointment scheduling, and mental health tracking can be incrementally developed, tested, and refined. Agile encourages collaboration, flexibility, and continuous improvement, reducing risk and improving responsiveness to change. Studies by Santos, da Silva, and Santos (2023) emphasize that Agile development enhances productivity and team performance, particularly when systems are complex and stakeholder needs evolve rapidly—as is common in academic institutions adapting to digital transformation.
Deployment. In Agile, this phase leverages Continuous Integration/Continuous Delivery (CI/CD) to ensure rapid, stable, and reliable updates. For a student-facing guidance system, this minimizes downtime and ensures that critical support services are always available. Frequent deployment cycles also allow for rapid integration of user feedback and timely delivery of improvements. A study by Al-Debagy and Martinek (2021) found that Agile-based deployment models improve software reliability and responsiveness, particularly in educational platforms where real-time updates and bug fixes are essential for uninterrupted service.

It defines system behavior from the user’s perspective and helps in capturing system requirements, outlining roles, and clarifying boundaries. This modeling approach plays a crucial role in analyzing how users engage with the system and what actions they can perform (Dennis, Wixom, & Tegarden, 2021).

3.2.1 Population and locale of the study
In this section, the developers outlined the population and locale relevant to the capstone project. This overview provided a clear context for the study, focusing on the demographic and geographical settings involved in the research.
Research Locale. This study was conducted at Pangasinan State University – San Carlos Campus, specifically focusing on the Guidance Office and involving students, employees’, and administrative staff from various departments. The researchers selected this location because it is directly related to their academic community and allows convenient access to the intended users of the guidance system. The San Carlos Campus was chosen due to observable challenges in delivering timely and effective student guidance services, including the manual processing of counseling schedules, limited tracking of student concerns, and inefficient dissemination of announcements. These existing gaps in the guidance process made the campus an ideal environment to implement and evaluate the effectiveness, usability, and impact of the Web-Based Guidance System in enhancing institutional support for student well-being and academic development.
Respondents of the Study. The respondents of this study were composed of selected users from Pangasinan State University – San Carlos Campus who were directly involved in the usage, evaluation, and administration of the proposed Web-Based Guidance System. To determine an appropriate number of participants, the researchers applied Slovin’s Formula with a 10% margin of error. This formula is commonly used to calculate a representative sample size when the total population is known and random sampling is intended. Using this approach, the computed sample size indicated that a minimum of 45 respondents would be adequate to provide reliable and meaningful insights regarding the system’s functionality, usability, and acceptability.
The final sample consisted of 45 respondents, distributed across three key user groups essential for evaluating the system comprehensively. The sample included one (1) administrator, representing 2% of the total respondents. The administrator’s perspective was vital since this role oversees the overall guidance operations and ensures proper implementation of institutional processes within the system.
Furthermore, three (3) college deans, accounting for 7%, also participated in the study. Their inclusion was significant as they possess administrative responsibilities and are actively engaged in monitoring student concerns, academic performance, and welfare-related matters that may require guidance interventions.
The largest portion of the respondents consisted of forty-one (41) students, making up 89% of the sample. Since students are the primary users of the guidance system, their feedback was essential in assessing system usability, accessibility, and overall user experience. Their participation provided firsthand insights into how effectively the system addressed their needs in scheduling appointments, accessing guidance services, and communicating with office personnel.
By incorporating multiple user groups and determining the sample size through Slovin’s Formula, the study ensured that the evaluation captured a balanced and representative set of perspectives. This approach strengthened the reliability of the findings and contributed to a more holistic assessment of the Web-Based Guidance System for Pangasinan State University – San Carlos Campus.
3.2.2 Data Instrumentation
This section describes the data collection methods utilized in the development of the Web-Based Guidance System for Pangasinan State University San Carlos Campus (PSUSC). These instruments were selected to ensure the reliability and validity of the findings, which were crucial in shaping the system’s functionality and relevance to user needs.
Focus Groups: Focus group discussions were organized with student representatives and guidance staff to gather qualitative data regarding their expectations and experiences with the current guidance services. This approach enabled developers to understand common concerns and preferences, which helped refine system features such as appointment scheduling, consultation tracking, and feedback mechanisms. According to Diluvi et al. (2023), focus groups provide flexible and rich insights that are particularly effective for gathering feedback in educational technology development, even in digital environment.
Internet Research: Internet-based research was employed to examine recent trends, user interface designs, and functional models of existing web-based guidance systems implemented in other academic institutions. This benchmarking activity helped identify best practices in providing digital mental health services, student engagement tools, and remote counseling features. Morales et al. (2021) emphasized the growing necessity of virtual guidance systems in higher education institutions, especially during and post-pandemic, to address accessibility and student well-being needs through technology-driven approaches.
Semi-Structured Interviews: Semi-structured interviews were conducted with university counselors, IT staff, and selected student users to delve deeper into their operational needs and technological expectations. This method allowed the developers to ask core questions while also exploring specific challenges or feedback shared by the participants. Ruslin et al. (2022) argued that semi-structured interviews offer a balance between consistency and flexibility, which makes them suitable for educational system research where adaptive questioning leads to more insightful findings.

3.4 Description of the Prototype
In this section, the prototype of the Web-Based Guidance System for Pangasinan State University San Carlos Campus is presented. This system is developed to enhance the administrative and guidance management processes by providing a centralized, accessible, and user-friendly digital platform. It is designed to streamline interactions between administrators, faculty, and students by offering core functionalities such as student information management, schedule tracking, and automated notifications. The system allows administrators and faculty to manage student records, publish announcements, and oversee training and assessment activities, while students can access their accounts to view personal profiles, class schedules, and important updates.
To visualize the system's operational flow and communication architecture, a network diagram is utilized. This diagram illustrates how users (admin, faculty, and students) interact with the system via smartphones and laptops through the internet, which connects to the web server and central database. The web server processes requests and communicates with the backend database to retrieve or store data, ensuring real-time performance and data integrity. Such visualization is critical in understanding the infrastructure of the web-based system, particularly in academic institutions where multiple stakeholders must interact with shared information systems.
According to Scott (2020), network diagrams are instrumental in system development as they help organizations clearly understand the components and data flow within a networked environment, identify potential inefficiencies, and facilitate better decision-making during deployment. Applying this concept to the Pangasinan State University San Carlos Campus, the network diagram ensures the clarity of system architecture, supporting the reliable and efficient implementation of the Web-Based Guidance System.
Figure 3.6. Network Diagram
The network diagram illustrates the architecture of the Web-Based Educational Information Management Support System for Pangasinan State University San Carlos Campus. It outlines the interaction between users (administrators, faculty, and students) and system components through internet-enabled devices such as laptops and smartphones. A centralized database supports the backend by storing essential data, including user profiles, schedules, and records. 3.5 The Proposed Implementation Plan
In this section, the developers present the proposed implementation plan for the Guidance System designed for Pangasinan State University – San Carlos Campus. This plan outlines a structured and systematic approach to deployment, ensuring that each phase—from initial planning to full operational use—is executed efficiently and effectively.
The primary goal of this study is to implement a web-based Guidance System that addresses the specific needs of the PSU–SCC Guidance Office. The system's features and content are designed to be flexible and responsive to the requirements of its users, including students, counselors, and administrative staff.
Initial stages of development involved conducting interviews and consultations with key stakeholders to gain a comprehensive understanding of the existing challenges and functional demands within the Guidance Office. These insights were analyzed to determine essential system requirements and to prioritize the development of features that directly support academic counseling, student tracking, and appointment management. This informed the design phase, where the development team worked collaboratively with stakeholders to produce an intuitive user interface and a stable system architecture.
The system was then developed with a focus on core functionalities such as secure user authentication, appointment scheduling, digital record-keeping for counseling sessions, and automated notifications for upcoming meetings and follow-ups. An iterative development approach was employed, involving regular feedback loops and refinements to ensure the system aligns with the operational workflows of the Guidance Office. Once testing was completed, the system was deployed in a live environment.
